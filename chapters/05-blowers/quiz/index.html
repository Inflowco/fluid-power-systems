    Quiz: Blowers and Pneumatic Conveying Systems

    Test your understanding of blower types, performance, wastewater aeration, pneumatic conveying, and system safety with these review questions.

    1. What pressure ratio range distinguishes a blower from a fan and a compressor?

    1. Below 1.11 (fan range)
    2. Above 1.20 (compressor range)
    3. 1.11 to 1.20 (between fan and compressor range)
    4. 1.50 to 2.00 (high-pressure range)
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is C. ASME defines blowers as machines operating at a pressure ratio of 1.11 to 1.20, which corresponds to a discharge pressure of approximately 1.5 to 15 psi. Fans operate below 1.11 (option A) and compressors operate above 1.20 (option B). This pressure gap is precisely where blowers are most energy-efficient — using a compressor for a 5–8 psi aeration application would waste enormous energy compressing air far beyond what is needed.

    2. How does a positive displacement (Roots) blower respond differently from a centrifugal blower when system pressure increases?

    1. The PD blower maintains nearly constant flow while the centrifugal blower delivers less flow
    2. Both types reduce flow equally as pressure increases
    3. The PD blower reduces flow while the centrifugal blower maintains constant flow
    4. Both types maintain constant flow regardless of pressure changes
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is A. A positive displacement blower traps and displaces a fixed volume of air per revolution regardless of discharge pressure — it simply draws more power as pressure rises. A centrifugal blower operates on a performance curve where increasing system pressure causes decreasing flow. This fundamental difference is why PD blowers dominate pneumatic conveying: if a pipeline develops a partial blockage (increasing back-pressure), a PD blower maintains airflow and can push through the blockage, while a centrifugal blower would lose airflow and potentially worsen the problem.

    3. A centrifugal blower operates at 3,000 RPM consuming 60 BHP. If the speed is reduced to 2,400 RPM using a VFD, what is the approximate new power consumption?

    1. 54 BHP — power drops by only 10% for a 20% speed reduction
    2. 48 BHP — power drops linearly with speed
    3. 38 BHP — power drops by the square of the speed ratio
    4. 31 BHP — power drops by the cube of the speed ratio
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is D. The third affinity law states that power is proportional to the cube of the speed ratio: W₂ = W₁ × (N₂/N₁)³ = 60 × (2,400/3,000)³ = 60 × (0.8)³ = 60 × 0.512 = 30.7 BHP. A 20% speed reduction yields approximately a 49% reduction in power. This cubic relationship is why VFD control is the single highest-return energy investment for centrifugal and turbo blower systems — throttling or bypass methods waste the energy instead of eliminating it.

    Concept Tested: Affinity Laws for Blowers

    4. What is surge in a multistage centrifugal blower, and why is it dangerous?

    1. A gradual loss of efficiency over time due to impeller erosion
    2. A condition where flow reverses direction, causing violent pressure oscillations and potential catastrophic mechanical damage
    3. A temporary increase in flow rate that causes vibration but no permanent damage
    4. An electrical overload condition caused by running the motor above rated speed
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. Surge occurs when airflow is reduced beyond the peak pressure point of the blower curve — pressure falls below the downstream pressure and air blows back into the blower. This causes extreme pulsation, greatly increased thrust loading on bearings, internal heat buildup that can close radial clearances (causing impellers to contact the casing), and potential shaft damage. Surge is not merely vibration (option C) — it can cause catastrophic failure. It is an aerodynamic phenomenon, not electrical (option D), and it occurs suddenly rather than gradually (option A).

    5. Why do fine-bubble diffusers deliver 2–4 times more oxygen per unit of air than coarse-bubble diffusers in wastewater aeration?

    1. Fine bubbles rise faster through the water, increasing contact time
    2. Fine-bubble diffusers operate at higher pressures, forcing more oxygen into solution
    3. Smaller bubbles have a much higher surface-area-to-volume ratio, providing more air-water contact
    4. Coarse-bubble diffusers lose most of their air through splashing at the water surface
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is C. Fine-bubble diffusers produce bubbles of 1–3 mm diameter compared to 6–50 mm for coarse-bubble diffusers. Smaller bubbles have a dramatically higher surface-area-to-volume ratio, which means much more contact area between air and water for oxygen transfer. Fine-bubble diffusers achieve oxygen transfer efficiencies of 15–30% per meter of submergence compared to only 4–8% for coarse-bubble diffusers. Upgrading from coarse-bubble to fine-bubble diffusers is one of the highest-return energy investments at a wastewater treatment plant.

    6. A pneumatic conveying system moves 8,000 lb/hr of material using 1,600 lb/hr of air. What is the solids loading ratio, and how is this system classified?

    1. SLR = 20, dense-phase conveying
    2. SLR = 5, dilute-phase conveying
    3. SLR = 0.2, very dilute conveying
    4. SLR = 5, dense-phase conveying
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is B. The solids loading ratio is the mass flow rate of solids divided by the mass flow rate of air: SLR = 8,000 / 1,600 = 5. An SLR of 1–15 classifies the system as dilute-phase conveying, where material particles are fully suspended in the air stream at high velocity (3,500–7,000 ft/min). Dense-phase conveying operates at SLR values of 15–50+ (option A), where material moves in concentrated slugs or plugs at much lower velocities. Option C incorrectly inverts the ratio.

    7. A pneumatic conveying system is experiencing 18% product breakage while conveying fragile breakfast cereal. Which combination of changes would most effectively reduce breakage to under 5%?

    1. Install HEPA filters at the destination and increase pulse cleaning frequency
    2. Switch from long-radius bends to short-radius bends and add more bends for gradual direction changes
    3. Add a larger blower and increase air velocity to move material faster through bends
    4. Convert to dense-phase conveying at low velocity and replace short-radius bends with long-radius or vortex elbows
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is D. Product breakage in pneumatic conveying is primarily caused by high-velocity particle impacts at bends. Converting from dilute-phase (5,500 ft/min) to dense-phase conveying (800–1,500 ft/min) reduces impact forces dramatically because impact energy scales with velocity squared. Replacing short-radius bends with long-radius or vortex elbows further reduces impact forces. Increasing velocity (option C) would worsen breakage. Adding filters (option A) addresses symptoms, not the root cause. Short-radius bends (option B) increase impact forces compared to long-radius bends.

    8. What makes high-speed turbo blowers the lowest total-cost-of-ownership option despite costing 2–3 times more than PD blowers upfront?

    1. They are the only blower type that can provide oil-free air for wastewater aeration
    2. They have the highest flow capacity of any blower type
    3. They deliver 30–40% energy savings plus near-zero maintenance costs through air-foil bearings and direct-drive design
    4. They are the quietest blower type and eliminate the need for acoustic enclosures
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is C. Over a 20-year blower lifecycle, energy accounts for 75% of total cost of ownership. Turbo blowers achieve 70–80% total package efficiency versus 50–65% for PD blowers, delivering 30–40% energy savings. Additionally, air-foil bearings eliminate oil changes, the direct-drive permanent-magnet motor eliminates belts and gears, and maintenance costs drop from $8,000–$12,000/yr to $1,000–$2,000/yr. Real-world case studies document payback periods of 2–4 years with 15–20 years of ongoing savings. While turbo blowers are also quiet and oil-free (options D and A), those features alone do not account for the lifecycle cost advantage.

    9. A pneumatic conveying system has 200 ft of horizontal pipe, 30 ft of vertical rise, and 6 long-radius bends (30 ft equivalent each). What is the total equivalent length?

    1. 260 ft — sum of horizontal and vertical lengths only
    2. 380 ft — horizontal plus vertical plus bend equivalents without doubling vertical
    3. 660 ft — all lengths tripled for safety factor
    4. 440 ft — horizontal plus doubled vertical plus bend equivalents
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is D. The equivalent length method converts all pipeline components to an equivalent length of straight horizontal pipe: L_eq = L_horizontal + (2 × L_vertical) + (n_bends × L_bend) = 200 + (2 × 30) + (6 × 30) = 200 + 60 + 180 = 440 ft. Vertical sections are doubled because lifting material against gravity requires approximately twice the pressure drop of horizontal conveying. Each bend is assigned its equivalent length based on type. The air mover must provide enough pressure for 440 ft of equivalent pipe, even though the physical length is only 230 ft.

    10. Which element of the dust explosion pentagon does a pneumatic conveying system NOT inherently provide, making it the critical factor to control?

    1. Ignition source — the system provides fuel, air, dispersion, and confinement but not a spark or heat source
    2. Dispersion — material moves as a solid stream, not as suspended dust
    3. Oxidant — the conveying air contains no oxygen
    4. Confinement — the system is open to atmosphere at both ends
    Show Answer

    The correct answer is A. Pneumatic conveying systems inherently satisfy three of the five conditions in the dust explosion pentagon: dispersion (dust is suspended in the air stream), confinement (enclosed pipelines and vessels), and oxidant (oxygen in the conveying air). If the material is combustible — and many common materials like sugar, flour, and sawdust are extremely explosive when dispersed in air — then only an ignition source is needed. This is why grounding and bonding (to prevent electrostatic sparks), spark detection systems, and proper housekeeping are critical safety requirements under NFPA 652 and 654. The 2008 Imperial Sugar explosion, which killed 14 workers, tragically demonstrated how dangerous combustible dust can be.
